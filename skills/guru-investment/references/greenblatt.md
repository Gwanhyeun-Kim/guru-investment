# Joel Greenblatt — Quantitative Value + Special Situations Master

## 1. Core Philosophy

Greenblatt occupies a unique position in the investing world: he is simultaneously a rigorous quantitative value investor and a deeply skilled event-driven special situations investor. These two sides are not contradictions — they share the same root insight: **markets misprice securities when human psychology and institutional constraints overwhelm rational analysis**.

The Magic Formula systematizes this insight across thousands of stocks. Special situations exploit it in concentrated, high-conviction bets where the mispricing is structural, not just statistical.

**Track Record**: Gotham Capital returned ~50% annualized gross (34.4% net) from 1985-1994. **The unifying principle**: "Figure out the value of something and then pay a lot less." Everything else — Magic Formula, spin-offs, LEAPS, merger securities — is a delivery mechanism for this single idea.

## 2. The Magic Formula — Complete Treatment

### 2a. The Two Metrics and Why These Specific Ones

**Metric 1 — Earnings Yield = EBIT / Enterprise Value**
- **Why EBIT, not net income**: Removes distortions from different capital structures (interest expense) and tax jurisdictions. Two identical businesses with different leverage would show different P/E ratios but the same EBIT/EV. Greenblatt wants to compare operating earning power on a level playing field.
- **Why Enterprise Value, not market cap**: EV represents what you'd actually pay to acquire the entire business — equity + debt - cash. A company trading at a $500M market cap with $1B in debt is actually a $1.5B acquisition. Using market cap alone makes levered companies look deceptively cheap.
- **Why not P/E ratio**: P/E double-counts leverage (higher interest lowers earnings AND is already reflected in lower stock price) and mixes operating performance with financial engineering.

**Metric 2 — Return on Invested Capital = EBIT / (Net Working Capital + Net Fixed Assets)**
- **Why tangible capital only**: Excludes goodwill from acquisitions because goodwill reflects what someone paid, not the actual capital needed to run the business. A company that overpaid for acquisitions shouldn't look like it has low returns on capital.
- **What it measures**: How efficiently the business converts physical capital into earnings. A business earning $10M on $20M of invested capital (50% ROIC) is fundamentally better than one earning $10M on $200M (5% ROIC).
- **Why not ROE**: ROE can be inflated by leverage. A company can show 30% ROE simply by taking on more debt. ROIC measures the actual business economics.

### 2b. Implementation Rules in Detail

**The Ranking System:**
1. Rank all eligible stocks by Earnings Yield (highest = rank #1)
2. Rank all eligible stocks by ROIC (highest = rank #1)
3. Add the two ranks together
4. Buy the 20-30 stocks with the lowest combined rank (i.e., top-ranked on both measures)

**Exclusions:**
- Financial companies (banks, insurance) — their capital structure makes ROIC meaningless
- Utilities — regulated returns distort ROIC
- Foreign ADRs (in the U.S. version) — data quality issues
- Minimum market cap filter: >$50M (broad universe) or >$1B (large-cap version)

**Staggered Entry and Tax Optimization (U.S.):**
- Buy 5-7 positions every 2-3 months until you hold 20-30 stocks
- Sell winners slightly AFTER 1 year (long-term capital gains treatment)
- Sell losers slightly BEFORE 1 year (short-term capital loss for maximum tax benefit)
- Replace each sold position with the next highest-ranked stock
- This staggering means you're always entering at different valuations, reducing timing risk

**Rebalancing Discipline:**
- Annual rebalancing per position (not the whole portfolio at once)
- Do NOT cherry-pick from the list — buy the top-ranked stocks mechanically
- Do NOT skip stocks because they "look bad" — that's the whole point

## 3. Special Situations — The Complete Playbook

This is the content of "You Can Be a Stock Market Genius" — arguably Greenblatt's most valuable book. While the Magic Formula is a passive system, special situations require active, concentrated, and deeply researched investing.

### 3a. Spin-offs

**Why spin-offs are systematically mispriced:**
- **Forced selling**: Index funds and institutions that hold the parent often must sell the spin-off (too small, wrong sector, not in their mandate). This selling is indiscriminate — based on institutional rules, not valuation.
- **Neglect**: Wall Street analysts don't cover small spin-offs. No coverage = no institutional buyers = depressed prices.
- **Information vacuum**: Limited financial history, no established analyst estimates, no track record as a standalone entity.
- **Shareholder disinterest**: Parent company shareholders receive shares they never asked for and often sell without analysis.

**What to look for in a spin-off:**
1. **Insider incentives** — Most important factor. Are management's options struck at or near the spin-off price? If executives own significant equity or have options at current prices, their interests are aligned with shareholders. This is the single strongest signal.
2. **Small spin-off from large parent** — The more size mismatch, the more selling pressure. A $500M spin-off from a $50B parent will face massive dumping.
3. **Spin-off has a different (better) business model** — If the parent is a conglomerate and the spin-off is a focused, high-ROIC business, the market may be valuing it at the parent's lower multiple.
4. **Institutional constraints cause forced selling** — Index reconstitution, mandate violations, minimum market cap requirements.
5. **The "ugly duckling"** — A spin-off that looks bad on the surface (high debt, messy financials) but has hidden value when you dig in.

**Research on spin-off performance:**
- Studies show spin-off stocks outperform the market by ~10% annually in the first 1-3 years
- Both the spin-off AND the remaining parent tend to outperform (parent is now more focused)
- The outperformance is concentrated in smaller spin-offs with high insider ownership

**Case Study: Marriott / Host Marriott (1993)**
- Marriott Corporation split into Marriott International (management/franchise fees) and Host Marriott (real estate assets + massive debt from 1980s hotel building spree)
- Host Marriott was the "ugly" piece — $25/share of debt against assets trading around $5/share
- Greenblatt's insight: Host's assets were worth ~$6/share, bought at $4/share = $2 margin of safety
- The leverage worked both ways: a 15% increase in asset values ($30 x 0.15 = $4.50) would nearly double the stock
- He also bought LEAPS options on the situation for additional leverage
- Result: Host traded to $6.75, Marriott International to $26, combined value $32.75 vs. ~$25 pre-split. His options returned 148% in 2 months. The stock tripled within 4 months.

**Case Study: Liberty Media / TCI (1990)**
- Tele-Communications Inc. spun off its programming assets (Liberty Media) under regulatory pressure
- CEO John Malone intentionally made the situation look complex — the messier it appeared, the lower the initial trading price, and the more stock his options would effectively cover
- Individual investors who participated in the rights offering earned 10x their investment in under two years
- Key lesson: When insiders structure things to acquire more equity, follow the insiders

**Case Study: Silver King (Liberty Media spin-off, 1993)**
- Traded at ~$5/share for its first four months at less than 5x cash flow
- No analyst coverage, institutional selling pressure from Liberty Media holders
- Appreciated to $10-$20 range once the forced selling subsided
- Classic example of a good business hidden inside a complex parent structure

### 3b. Restructurings and Post-Bankruptcy Equities

**Why post-bankruptcy stocks are mispriced:**
- New equity is typically distributed to former creditors (bondholders, banks) as part of the reorganization plan
- These creditors are debt investors — they don't want to own equity and sell immediately
- The company emerges with a clean balance sheet, reduced debt, and often a new management team
- No analyst coverage initially; too small or too "tainted" for most institutional investors

**What to look for:**
- Clean balance sheet post-emergence
- Strong underlying business that was overleveraged, not fundamentally broken
- New management with meaningful equity incentives
- Former creditors dumping shares (creates the buying opportunity)
- Simplified corporate structure

**Greenblatt's key insight**: "It's not the company that went bankrupt that interests me — it's what the company looks like when it comes out the other side."

### 3c. Merger Securities (Not Traditional Risk Arbitrage)

**Greenblatt's critical distinction**: He is skeptical of traditional merger arbitrage (buying target at $48 hoping to collect $50 at deal close). The risk/reward is unfavorable — you make $1-2 if the deal closes but lose $10-20 if it breaks.

**Where the real opportunity lies — merger securities:**
- In many mergers, target shareholders receive not just cash but also securities (bonds, preferred stock, warrants, stub equity in the acquirer)
- These merger securities get dumped indiscriminately because:
  - Individual investors receive instruments they don't understand
  - Equity fund managers can't hold bonds/preferreds (mandate restrictions)
  - The amounts are often too small for institutional interest
- Result: merger securities frequently trade at significant discounts to intrinsic value

**Framework for evaluating merger securities:**
- What is the security actually worth on fundamental analysis?
- Why are holders selling? (Forced/institutional vs. fundamental concerns)
- What is the downside if the deal breaks? (Some merger securities retain value regardless)
- Is there a catalyst for price recovery?

### 3d. Rights Offerings and Recapitalizations

**Rights offerings:**
- Companies issue rights to existing shareholders to buy additional shares, usually at a discount
- Many shareholders don't participate (don't understand, don't want to invest more, too small to bother)
- Oversubscription privileges allow those who do participate to buy the unexercised shares — often at substantial discounts
- The complexity and small size of rights offerings drives away institutional investors

**Recapitalizations and stub stocks:**
- When a company does a leveraged recapitalization (borrows heavily to pay a large special dividend or buy back stock), the remaining equity becomes a "stub"
- The stub is highly leveraged — small changes in business value create large percentage changes in equity value
- If the underlying business is good, the stub offers enormous upside
- Greenblatt: Stub stocks "amplify companies' increases in earnings while limiting downside risk" — though this is only true if the underlying business value exceeds the debt

**Sears / Dean Witter / Allstate Case:**
- Sears partially spun off Dean Witter (stock valued at $37) and Allstate ($29)
- For every Sears share, shareholders received 0.4 DW shares + 1 Allstate share
- Simple math showed the combined value of pieces exceeded Sears' trading price of $54
- The "complexity" of tracking three securities caused many investors to miss the straightforward sum-of-parts arithmetic

### 3e. LEAPS as Value Investing Tools

**Greenblatt's framework**: LEAPS (long-term equity anticipation securities) are NOT speculative tools in his hands — they are a way to structure a value investment with defined risk and leveraged upside.

**When to use LEAPS instead of stock:**
- You have high conviction in the thesis
- There is a specific catalyst (spin-off, restructuring, earnings recovery) within the LEAPS timeframe
- The risk/reward ratio with LEAPS is dramatically better than owning the stock outright
- You want to limit maximum loss to the premium paid

**Case Study: Wells Fargo LEAPS (1992-1994)**
- Context: California real estate crisis. Wells Fargo had the largest concentration of CA real estate loans. Stock at $77, market feared insolvency.
- Greenblatt's analysis: Adjusting for cash earnings and one-time expenses, Wells was earning $36/share pre-tax. Normalized loan-loss provisions would be ~$6/share. Normalized after-tax earnings: ~$18/share. At 9-10x P/E = $160-180 fair value.
- The LEAPS trade: Bought January 1995 calls, $80 strike, paid $14 premium
- Risk/reward: Max loss = $14. If Wells recovered to $160, LEAPS worth $80. Risk/reward = 1:5
- Outcome: Wells earned $15/share in 1994, $20/share in 1995. Stock hit $160 by September 1994. LEAPS returned ~500% (nearly 5x the premium paid).
- Key lesson: The LEAPS structure meant Greenblatt could invest in a "risky" situation with a defined, limited downside while capturing most of the upside.

**Greenblatt's rules for LEAPS:**
1. Only use when you have a specific catalyst and strong conviction
2. Buy deep-in-the-money or at-the-money LEAPS for value situations (not far out-of-the-money speculative bets)
3. Think of the premium as your maximum risk — if you're not comfortable losing it entirely, size smaller
4. "There is almost no other area of the stock market where research and careful analysis can be rewarded as quickly and as generously"

### 3f. Stub Stocks

**What they are**: When a company owns a publicly traded subsidiary, you can calculate the "stub" value: parent market cap minus the market value of its stake in the subsidiary. Sometimes the stub trades at zero or negative — meaning the market is giving you the parent's other businesses for free.

**Why they're mispriced:**
- Requires sum-of-parts thinking that most investors don't do
- Complex corporate structures discourage analysis
- If the subsidiary is more "exciting" than the parent, capital flows to the subsidiary and ignores the parent

**Greenblatt's approach:**
- Calculate the stub value precisely
- Ask: "Am I getting the remaining businesses for a reasonable price?"
- If the stub is at zero or negative, ask: "What would have to go wrong for the remaining businesses to actually be worth nothing?"
- The margin of safety in stub situations can be enormous

## 4. The Behavioral Edge — Why Value Investing Works

Greenblatt's explanation of why value investing works is rooted in behavioral finance and institutional structure, not market theory:

**Human Psychology:**
- **Loss aversion**: Investors feel losses ~2x more intensely than gains. They sell winners too early and hold losers too long. Value stocks feel like "losers" — they're cheap because something went wrong.
- **Recency bias**: Recent poor performance is extrapolated indefinitely. A company that had 2 bad quarters "will never recover."
- **Herding**: Professional investors cluster around consensus picks. Buying a stock no one else owns is career risk.
- **Short-term focus**: The average holding period for stocks has shrunk to months. Value investing requires years of patience.

**Institutional Constraints:**
- **Index fund mandates**: Must sell spin-offs that fall below minimum size or exit the index
- **Style box restrictions**: A "large-cap growth" fund cannot hold a small-cap value spin-off
- **Career risk**: Fund managers who underperform for 2 years get fired. They can't afford to hold "ugly" cheap stocks even if the expected return is high.
- **Quarterly reporting**: Managers window-dress portfolios, selling embarrassing holdings before quarter-end

**Greenblatt's key lecture point**: "Stock prices are very volatile over short periods of time, but it does not make sense that values are nearly as volatile as the prices — and therein lies what should be a great opportunity."

> "You can't be a good value investor without being an independent thinker — you're seeing valuations that the market is not appreciating. But it's critical that you understand why the market isn't seeing the value you do."

## 5. Valuation Framework Beyond Magic Formula

When Greenblatt analyzes individual stocks (not screening), he uses a multi-layered valuation approach:

### 5a. Normalized Earnings
- Do NOT use peak or trough earnings
- Estimate what the company will earn across a full business cycle
- Adjust for one-time charges, restructuring costs, and cyclical extremes
- Ask: "What will this business earn in a normal year, 2-3 years from now?"

### 5b. Sum-of-Parts Analysis
- Especially critical for conglomerates, holding companies, and companies with diverse divisions
- Value each business segment separately using appropriate peer multiples
- Add the parts together and compare to the current market cap
- If parts > whole, there's a potential catalyst if the company spins off, sells, or restructures

### 5c. Private Market Value
- What would a rational private buyer pay for the entire business?
- Look at comparable M&A transactions in the sector
- Private buyers typically pay a premium over public market prices because they can extract synergies and control the capital allocation
- If the stock trades at a large discount to private market value, you have a margin of safety

### 5d. DCF with Conservative Assumptions
- Greenblatt uses DCF but cautions against false precision
- "If you need to go out to the fourth decimal place, it's too close to call"
- Use conservative growth rates, reasonable terminal multiples, and high discount rates
- The DCF should confirm what the simpler metrics already suggest — not be the primary basis for a thesis

### 5e. The Simplicity Test
- "If an investment idea is truly strong, you should be able to explain why it's a great business, why it's temporarily undervalued, and why it should be worth more in the future"
- "The most successful investments are often those that, after thorough research, seem obvious and straightforward"
- If you need complex models to justify the investment, it's probably not a Greenblatt-style opportunity

## 8. Columbia Business School — Key Lecture Insights

Greenblatt has taught Special Situation Investing at Columbia since the early 2000s. Class notes from 2002-2006 are widely circulated. Key themes:

1. **"Prices are not the same as values"**: Stock prices are far more volatile than underlying business values. This gap is your opportunity.

2. **Concentration vs. diversification is a personal question**: If you do the work, 5-8 stocks is enough. If you can't handle the volatility, you need more positions. Neither approach is "right."

3. **Do your own work**: "The secret to successful investing is relatively simple — figure out the value of something and then pay a lot less." But you have to do the figuring yourself.

4. **Look where others don't**: The best opportunities are in the "nooks and crannies" of the market — spin-offs, post-bankruptcy, rights offerings, complex capital structures. These areas are under-researched by design.

5. **The most important quality is temperament, not intellect**: "Markets don't just move on facts; they move on sentiment, groupthink, fear, and FOMO. If you can stay rational while others react, opportunity arises."

6. **Normalized earnings are the key to valuation**: Don't value a cyclical company at peak or trough earnings. Estimate mid-cycle earning power, apply a reasonable multiple, and compare to the current price.

7. **Insiders are the best signal**: In special situations, follow the insiders. Their incentive structure tells you more than any analyst report.

8. **Position sizing should reflect conviction AND liquidity**: "When we like a company we size the positions as big as we can, given the liquidity constraint."

## 9. Common Misunderstandings About Greenblatt

1. **"He's just a quant"** — Wrong. His best returns came from concentrated, qualitative special situations investing at Gotham Capital. The Magic Formula came later as a way to systematize the insight for regular investors.

2. **"The Magic Formula is stock picking"** — Wrong. It's a mechanical system. The moment you override it, you destroy the edge.

3. **"Special situations are too complex for individual investors"** — Greenblatt wrote "You Can Be a Stock Market Genius" specifically to argue the opposite. Individual investors have ADVANTAGES over institutions in special situations: no mandates, no career risk, no minimum position sizes.

4. **"High ROIC = good stock"** — Not by itself. The formula requires BOTH high ROIC and high earnings yield. A great business at an expensive price is not a Magic Formula buy.

5. **"The formula stopped working"** — It has periods of underperformance, which is precisely why it works long-term. Recent underperformance in U.S. large caps may also reflect the growth/momentum supercycle and quant crowding.

6. **"Greenblatt is a buy-and-hold investor"** — Not exactly. His Magic Formula rebalances annually. His special situations have specific catalysts and timelines. He's a value investor with a time horizon, not a "forever" holder.

7. **"You need to pick between the Magic Formula and special situations"** — Greenblatt himself does both. The Magic Formula is the systematic base; special situations are the opportunistic overlay.

## 11. The Big Secret for the Small Investor — Value-Weighted Indexing

Greenblatt's third book (2011) argues for a middle ground between passive indexing and active stock picking:

**The problem with cap-weighted indexes:**
- Cap-weighted indexes (S&P 500, Russell 3000) automatically overweight expensive stocks and underweight cheap ones
- As a stock's price rises (potentially beyond fair value), its weight in the index increases — you buy more of what's expensive
- This is a systematic drag on returns

**The solution — value-weighted indexing:**
- Weight stocks by "cheapness" (earnings yield) and "quality" (return on capital) — essentially applying Magic Formula principles to index construction
- Own more of the bargain stocks, less of the overpriced ones
- Backtest showed 4-6% annual improvement over cap-weighted indexing with similar risk/volatility

**Practical implication**: For investors who don't want to pick individual stocks OR follow the Magic Formula mechanically, a fundamentally-weighted value index is Greenblatt's recommended passive approach.

## 12. How to Apply to Korean Stocks

### Magic Formula Screening for KOSPI/KOSDAQ
- Screen by EBIT/EV and ROIC, exclude financials (banks, insurance, securities firms)
- Korean data sources: DART (financial statements), KRX (market data), FnGuide (screening)
- Apply minimum liquidity filter — many Korean value stocks have near-zero liquidity, creating "value traps you can't exit"
- Korean small caps frequently trade at extremely high earnings yields — deep value territory

### Korean Corporate Events as Greenblatt-Style Special Situations

**Physical Spin-offs (물적분할):**
- Parent retains 100% ownership of the new subsidiary
- Often value-destructive for minority shareholders (subsidiary IPO captures value that parent shareholders lose)
- Greenblatt framework: Analyze whether the spin-off creates forced selling, insider incentive misalignment, or sum-of-parts discount
- Recent controversy: LG Chem / LG Energy Solution, SK Innovation / SK On — minority shareholders protested value transfer

**Organizational Spin-offs (인적분할):**
- Shareholders of the parent receive shares in both the parent and the new entity proportionally
- More analogous to U.S.-style spin-offs — creates forced selling and neglect dynamics
- Look for: Small spin-off from large chaebol parent, institutional dumping, insider incentive structure

**Holding Company Conversions (지주회사 전환):**
- Korean conglomerate restructuring into holding company + operating subsidiaries
- Creates Greenblatt-style sum-of-parts discount — holding company trades at 30-60% discount to NAV
- Greenblatt would analyze: What are the parts actually worth? What is the stub (holdco value minus listed subsidiary stakes)?
- The "Korea Discount" means holdco discounts are often wider than in other markets

**Chaebol Mergers and Restructuring (합병, 분할, 자사주 소각):**
- Cross-shareholding unwinds = forced selling dynamic
- Treasury stock cancellation = capital return event (increasing EPS, reducing EV)
- Related-party mergers can be either value-creative or value-destructive — must analyze the merger ratio and who benefits

**Value-Up Program (기업 밸류업 프로그램):**
- Government initiative encouraging companies to improve capital allocation, increase dividends, reduce cross-shareholdings
- Companies improving capital efficiency = rising ROIC = Magic Formula starts to pick them up
- The program creates a systematic catalyst for value realization across the Korean market
- Two-thirds of Korean companies trade below book value — enormous potential value unlock

### Practical Korean Application
- Use DART filings (사업보고서) for EBIT, invested capital, enterprise value calculations
- Monitor KRX corporate action announcements for spin-offs, rights offerings, mergers
- Korean holdco discount screens: Calculate NAV of listed subsidiaries, compare to holdco market cap
- Watch for chaebol succession events — ownership restructuring often creates special situations
- Be aware: Korean corporate governance is improving but still features related-party transactions that can destroy minority shareholder value. The Greenblatt framework works, but requires additional governance analysis.

## Key Metrics Quick Reference

| Metric | Formula | Purpose |
|--------|---------|---------|
| Earnings Yield | EBIT / Enterprise Value | Cheapness (higher = cheaper) |
| Return on Invested Capital | EBIT / (NWC + Net Fixed Assets) | Quality (higher = better business) |
| Magic Formula Rank | EY Rank + ROIC Rank | Combined score (lower = better) |
| Stub Value | Parent Market Cap - Value of Listed Subsidiaries | Hidden value in holdcos |
| Normalized Earnings | Mid-cycle EBIT estimate | Through-cycle earning power |
| Private Market Value | Comparable M&A transaction multiples | Floor valuation |

## What Greenblatt Would Avoid
- Low ROIC businesses, even if statistically cheap (capital destroyers)
- Companies where "cheap" = structural decline with no catalyst
- Overcomplicating the analysis — if it's not obvious after the work, move on
- Trying to time the Magic Formula (just stay invested, rebalance annually)
- Financial and utility stocks (ROIC framework doesn't apply)
- Traditional risk arbitrage (asymmetric downside)
- Special situations without clear insider alignment
- Any investment where the thesis requires complex models to justify
