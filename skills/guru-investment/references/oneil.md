# William O'Neil -- The CANSLIM System: Complete Reference

## Background

William Joseph O'Neil (1933-2023). Born in Oklahoma City. Studied business at Southern Methodist University (BA 1955). Served in the U.S. Air Force. Began career as a stockbroker at Hayden, Stone & Company in 1958. Early investment in Syntex Corporation (developer of the birth control pill) turned $5,000 into $200,000+ in a single year. Bought a seat on the New York Stock Exchange at age 30 -- the youngest person to do so at that time. Founded William O'Neil & Co. Inc. (institutional research firm) in 1963. Launched a mutual fund that achieved 115% return in 1967. Founded Investor's Business Daily (IBD) in 1984 as a data-driven alternative to The Wall Street Journal. Author of "How to Make Money in Stocks" (1988, 4th ed. 2011), "The Successful Investor" (2004), and "24 Essential Lessons for Investment Success" (2000). Created the CAN SLIM investment strategy -- named the top-performing investment strategy from 1998 to 2009 by the American Association of Individual Investors (AAII). Died May 28, 2023.

---

## 1. Core Philosophy

O'Neil's approach is empirical, not theoretical. He did not start with an investing philosophy and look for stocks that fit it. He started with the data: what did the greatest stock market winners actually look like BEFORE they made their major price advances?

**Key principles:**
- Study history. The market repeats because human nature does not change.
- Every great stock shares specific fundamental AND technical characteristics before its move.
- Stock selection is necessary but not sufficient. Market timing and sell discipline are equally important.
- Losses must be cut mechanically. Ego is the enemy of good investing.
- Concentrate in the best ideas. Diversification is a hedge for ignorance.
- Buy high, sell higher. Stocks making new highs tend to go higher; stocks making new lows tend to go lower.
- Three out of four stocks follow the general market direction. Getting the market wrong makes stock selection almost irrelevant.

**What makes O'Neil unique:** He combined fundamental analysis (earnings, sales, ROE) with technical analysis (chart patterns, volume, relative strength) and market timing (follow-through days, distribution days) into a single integrated system. No other major investor has systematized all three with equal rigor.

## 2. The Model Book Studies -- O'Neil's Foundation

O'Neil's entire methodology is built on exhaustive historical research of stock market winners, not on theory.

**The research progression:**
- **1st edition (1988):** Studied 500 of the greatest stock market winners from 1953 to 1993
- **"The Model Book of Greatest Stock Market Winners" (1970s-1980s):** Catalogued 272 stocks that at least doubled between 1970 and 1983 (~19 per year), documenting their fundamental and technical characteristics
- **4th edition (2011):** Extended analysis to 1,000+ top-performing stocks from 1880 through 2009 -- covering over 130 years of market history

**What the studies found -- common characteristics BEFORE the big move:**

| Characteristic | Finding |
|---|---|
| Quarterly EPS growth | Average 70% increase in the quarter before the major advance |
| Annual EPS growth | 25%+ per year for the prior 3 years in the vast majority |
| Something "New" | 95% had a new product, service, management, or industry condition |
| Relative price strength | Average RS Rating of 87 just before the winning run began |
| Institutional sponsorship | Increasing number of quality institutional holders |
| Small to mid float | Most were not mega-caps; tighter supply amplified demand |
| Chart base pattern | Nearly all formed recognizable base patterns (cup-with-handle, double bottom, flat base) before breaking out |
| Market direction | Most major winners emerged early in new bull markets, often within the first 12-15 months after a follow-through day |

**The model book approach in practice:** O'Neil kept physical binders of annotated stock charts going back to the 1880s. He studied every major winner -- Northern Pacific (1901), General Motors (1913-14), RCA (1920s), Syntex (1963), Dome Petroleum (1978), Cisco (1990), AOL (1998), Google (2004), Apple (2004-2012), Monster Beverage (2003-2012). The point was not prediction but pattern recognition: if you know what a great stock looks like before it moves, you can identify the next one in real time.

## 3. CANSLIM -- Deep Dive

Each letter represents one of the seven characteristics shared by history's greatest stock winners. All seven should ideally be present simultaneously.

### C -- Current Quarterly Earnings Per Share

**Core rule:** Current quarterly EPS must be up at least 25% versus the same quarter last year.

**Deeper detail:**
- The best-performing stocks averaged a 70% quarterly EPS increase before their major advance
- Ideal: 50-100%+ acceleration
- Look for ACCELERATION: 20% -> 30% -> 50% -> 80% (each quarter faster than the last)
- Deceleration is a warning sign: 80% -> 50% -> 30% suggests the growth story is maturing

**Quality checks:**
- Revenue must also be growing at least 25%. Earnings growth without revenue growth = cost-cutting, margin expansion, or financial engineering. These are unsustainable.
- Check that EPS growth is from core operations, not one-time gains (asset sales, tax benefits, accounting changes)
- Compare to analyst estimates: beating estimates by a wide margin is bullish; meeting or missing after a history of beats is bearish
- Operating margin expansion alongside revenue growth = the ideal combination

**Traps to avoid:**
- Companies with lumpy, cyclical earnings can show big YoY percentage gains off a depressed base. The absolute direction matters as much as the percentage.
- Very high EPS growth (500%+) off near-zero earnings is less meaningful than 70% growth off a solid base
- Watch for earnings driven by share buybacks rather than true operating growth

### A -- Annual Earnings Increases

**Core rule:** Annual EPS should have increased each of the last 3 consecutive years, at a rate of 25%+ per year.

**Deeper detail:**
- Consistency matters: 30%, 35%, 40% annual growth is better than 5%, 80%, 20%
- Three-year track record screens out one-hit wonders and cyclical bounces
- ROE should be at least 17% (demonstrates efficient capital deployment)
- The best winners often had ROE of 25-50%

**Cash flow verification:**
- Annual cash flow per share should exceed EPS (earnings quality check)
- Growing free cash flow confirms the business is generating real economic value, not just accounting profits
- Be wary of companies where EPS is growing but operating cash flow is flat or declining

**Additional checks:**
- Annual pre-tax profit margins should be expanding or at industry-leading levels
- Debt/equity ratio should be declining or manageable
- Stability of growth: an annual EPS stability factor (lower is better) helps identify consistent growers versus volatile earners

### N -- New Products, New Management, New Price Highs

**Core rule:** 95% of the greatest winners had something fundamentally "new" driving their growth.

**The three types of "new":**
1. **New product or service:** A genuine innovation that creates or captures a large market. Examples: Syntex (birth control pill, 1963), Apple (iPhone, 2007), Google (search advertising, 2004)
2. **New management:** Fresh leadership with a clear strategic vision that unlocks value. Turnarounds driven by a new CEO can produce massive moves.
3. **New industry condition:** A structural shift in the industry -- deregulation, technological disruption, demographic change

**Why "new" matters:** Growth stocks are growth stocks because something specific is driving accelerating demand. Without a catalyst, earnings cannot sustain the 25%+ quarterly gains CANSLIM requires. The "N" forces the investor to identify WHY the stock is growing, not just that it is.

**New price highs:**
- Buy stocks emerging from sound base patterns into new price highs, NOT stocks making new lows
- "It seems like a paradox, but stocks that appear too high usually go higher, and stocks that appear cheap usually go lower."
- A stock breaking to a new 52-week or all-time high from a proper base is eliminating overhead supply (no trapped shareholders above to sell into rallies)

### S -- Supply and Demand

**Core rule:** Favor stocks with smaller float and look for signs of accumulation (institutional buying).

**Float analysis:**
- Smaller float = fewer shares available = less buying pressure needed to drive price up
- Under 25-50 million shares is ideal for explosive moves
- Mega-cap stocks with billions of shares outstanding can still work, but they move more slowly and require massive institutional buying to advance
- Small float + high growth + institutional discovery = the highest-potential setup

**Volume analysis (accumulation vs. distribution):**
- **Accumulation:** Above-average volume on up days, below-average volume on down days. This indicates institutions are quietly buying.
- **Distribution:** Above-average volume on down days, below-average volume on up days. Institutions are selling.
- Track the Accumulation/Distribution Rating (A through E scale): A or B rating = institutions are net buyers
- On breakout day, volume should be at least 40-50% above the 50-day average. This confirms institutional sponsorship of the move.

**Share buybacks:** Company reducing share count = positive supply/demand dynamic. Management is confident enough to buy back stock, and fewer shares amplify EPS growth.

### L -- Leader or Laggard

**Core rule:** Buy only market leaders. A stock's Relative Strength (RS) Rating must be 80 or higher.

**Relative Strength methodology:**
- RS Rating ranks a stock's price performance over the past 12 months relative to all other stocks, on a 1-99 scale
- The formula weights the most recent 3-month period at 40%, and each of the prior three quarters at 20%
- RS 80 = the stock is outperforming 80% of all other stocks
- RS 85-95 = the sweet spot for the strongest candidates
- The average RS Rating of the best-performing stocks just prior to their major advance was 87 (IBD research, 1950-2008)

**Why RS is the single most important screen:**
- A stock cannot become a major winner if it is not already outperforming the market. Relative strength is the market's verdict on a stock's quality.
- RS declines before price declines. If a stock's RS line starts making lower highs while price holds, it is weakening relative to the market even if the chart looks fine in absolute terms.
- RS new highs before price new highs = extremely bullish confirmation

**Industry group analysis:**
- O'Neil identified 197 industry groups and ranked them by price performance
- Buy stocks in the top 20-30% ranked industry groups
- "Buy the #1 company in the #1 industry group"
- Group strength often precedes individual stock moves: when a group starts outperforming, the leaders within that group are the best candidates
- About 50% of a stock's price movement is attributable to its industry group and sector

### I -- Institutional Sponsorship

**Core rule:** The stock should have increasing institutional sponsorship from quality funds.

**Quality over quantity:**
- A few top-performing mutual funds or institutions owning the stock > hundreds of mediocre ones
- Look for ownership by funds with strong long-term track records (top quartile performance over 3-5 years)
- Funds managed by star portfolio managers who have demonstrated the ability to pick winners

**The "smart money" filter:**
- Track quarter-over-quarter changes in institutional holders: the number should be increasing
- New positions initiated by top-performing funds = strongest signal
- Watch for funds increasing existing positions (adding shares)

**When institutional sponsorship becomes a negative:**
- If every major institution already owns the stock, who is left to buy? Saturation of institutional ownership means the stock is widely known and the "discovery" phase is over.
- When a stock appears in every top-10 holdings list, it is more likely at a late stage than an early stage
- The ideal setup: a stock transitioning from little institutional ownership to rapidly increasing institutional interest

### M -- Market Direction (THE Most Important Factor)

**Core rule:** Three out of four stocks follow the general market direction. Even the best CANSLIM stock will fail in a bear market.

This is the most underweighted factor by most investors and the most important in O'Neil's system. See Section 5 (Market Timing) for the complete mechanical system.

**The three market states (IBD classification):**
1. **Confirmed Uptrend:** Aggressive buying permitted. Deploy 80-100% of capital in the best CANSLIM stocks.
2. **Uptrend Under Pressure:** Caution. Take partial profits, tighten stops, halt new buying or limit to exceptional setups only. Reduce exposure to 50-75%.
3. **Market in Correction:** Defensive mode. Reduce exposure to 0-25%. Cash is a position. Do NOT buy breakouts in a correction -- the failure rate is dramatically higher.

## 4. Chart Patterns -- The Complete O'Neil Toolkit

O'Neil's model book studies revealed that virtually every great stock winner formed a recognizable base pattern before breaking out. Base patterns represent periods of consolidation where weak holders sell and strong holders accumulate.

### Base Counting (Stage Analysis)

Every stock cycles through bases. O'Neil counts them:
- **1st stage base:** Highest probability of success. The stock is relatively unknown, emerging from a long consolidation or IPO base.
- **2nd stage base:** Still high probability. The stock has made its first major advance and is consolidating gains before the next leg up.
- **3rd stage base:** Lower probability. The stock is becoming widely known. More risk of failure.
- **4th stage base and beyond:** Significantly higher failure rate. The stock is late-stage, widely owned, and running out of new buyers. Most climax tops occur after 3rd or 4th stage bases.
- **Reset rule:** If a stock falls far enough to undercut the lows of its prior base, the base count resets to stage 1.

### Cup-With-Handle (O'Neil's Signature Pattern)

**Cup characteristics:**
- Shape: U-shaped (rounded bottom). NOT V-shaped -- a V-bottom is too sharp and does not allow sufficient time for weak holders to sell
- Depth: Ideally 15-33% correction from peak to trough. Can extend to 40-50% in severe bear markets, but deeper than 50% = structurally damaged
- Duration: 7-65 weeks (most common: 3-6 months). Shorter than 7 weeks = too immature
- Volume: Should dry up (decline) at the bottom of the cup, indicating selling pressure has exhausted itself. Volume should increase as the right side of the cup forms (accumulation)
- The right side of the cup should ideally recover to within 5-15% of the left-side high before the handle forms

**Handle characteristics:**
- Shape: Slight downward drift on declining volume. This is the final shakeout of remaining weak holders.
- Depth: 5-15% pullback from the high of the right side of the cup. Should not exceed more than 1/3 of the cup's total depth.
- Duration: Typically 1-4 weeks, can be longer
- Volume: Must decrease during the handle formation. Volume dry-up is critical -- it means sellers have been exhausted.
- Position: The handle should form in the upper half of the overall base pattern. A handle forming in the lower half is weak.

**The pivot (buy point):**
- The highest point of the handle = the pivot point (buy point)
- Add $0.10 to the pivot to get the exact trigger price
- Breakout must occur on volume at least 40-50% above the 50-day average
- The buy zone extends to 5% above the pivot point. Beyond 5%, the stock is extended and should NOT be chased.

**Faulty cup-with-handle characteristics (avoid these):**
- V-shaped cup (too sharp, no consolidation time)
- Cup depth greater than 2.5x the general market's decline (too deep, structurally damaged)
- Wedging handle (handle drifts upward instead of downward -- does not produce a proper shakeout)
- Handle forms in the lower half of the base
- Handle too deep (more than 1/3 of cup depth)
- Wide and loose price action (erratic, high-volatility swings throughout the base)
- Volume increases during the handle (sellers are active, not exhausted)
- Breakout on low volume (no institutional conviction)

### Double Bottom

- Shape: Resembles the letter "W"
- Minimum duration: 7 weeks
- Maximum depth: typically under 40%
- The second low MUST undercut the first low (this is the shakeout that creates the proper setup -- it traps sellers and clears out weak holders)
- The buy point: the middle peak of the "W" (the rally high between the two lows), plus $0.10
- Volume should dry up on the second low and surge on the breakout above the middle peak
- A double bottom where the second low is higher than the first is weaker and has a lower success rate

### Flat Base

- Shape: Tight, shallow, sideways consolidation
- Depth: No more than 10-15% from high to low
- Duration: Minimum 5 weeks
- Typically forms AFTER a stock has already advanced 20%+ from a prior breakout (it is a consolidation of gains, not a bottoming pattern)
- Prior uptrend requirement: at least 30% advance before the flat base begins
- Buy point: the high of the flat base structure, plus $0.10
- Flat bases indicate exceptional strength: the stock refuses to pull back meaningfully despite general market weakness or time passing
- Often leads to the next leg of a major advance

### High Tight Flag (Rare but Powerful)

- The rarest and most powerful chart pattern
- Prerequisite: Stock must have run up 100%+ in 4-8 weeks (the "flagpole")
- The flag: a tight correction of 10-25% over 3-5 weeks
- Buy point: the high of the flag, plus $0.10
- These are exceptionally strong stocks. The 100%+ run in a short time signals massive institutional demand.
- O'Neil noted these produce the biggest winners but are rare (a few per year in the entire market)

### Base-on-Base

- A new base forms directly on top of a prior base (the stock did not advance far enough -- less than 20% -- before forming a new base)
- Usually occurs when the general market corrects during the stock's initial advance, preventing it from completing a full move
- The combined structure (two bases stacked) can be very powerful because the stock has been consolidating for an extended period while building a very deep foundation of support
- Buy point: the high of the second (upper) base, plus $0.10

### Ascending Base

- Three pullbacks to higher support levels, each of 10-20%
- Each low is higher than the prior low
- Total formation: typically 9-16 weeks
- Indicates persistent accumulation even during minor corrections

## 5. Market Timing -- The Mechanical System

This is the section most investors underweight, and it is the section O'Neil considered most important. His market timing system is entirely mechanical -- it removes opinion, prediction, and emotion.

### Follow-Through Day (FTD) -- Confirming a New Uptrend

**Definition:** A follow-through day occurs on Day 4 or later of a rally attempt, when a major market index (Nasdaq Composite or S&P 500) rises at least 1.25-1.7% on volume higher than the prior day.

**The mechanics:**
1. The market has been in a confirmed correction or downtrend.
2. A rally attempt begins: the index makes an intraday low, then rallies. Day 1 = first day of the rally attempt (the day the index attempts to rally off a low).
3. Days 2-3: The index must hold above the Day 1 low. It does NOT need to advance, but it must NOT undercut the Day 1 low. If it does, the rally attempt fails and the count resets.
4. Day 4 or later: A major index closes up at least 1.25-1.7% on volume higher than the previous session. THIS is the follow-through day.
5. A follow-through day signals: institutions are beginning to commit capital to the long side.

**Minimum gain threshold evolution:**
- O'Neil originally required 1.0% gain
- Later raised to 1.25%, then 1.5%, then 1.7% as institutional awareness of the signal increased
- Bigger FTDs (2%+) on much higher volume are more reliable

**Reliability:**
- O'Neil reported approximately 75-80% reliability for follow-through days
- Critical insight: NOT every FTD leads to a sustained bull market, BUT no major bull market has ever started without one
- The FTD is a NECESSARY but NOT SUFFICIENT condition for a new uptrend
- FTDs that occur on Day 4-7 of a rally attempt are generally more reliable than FTDs on Day 10+

**How to confirm an FTD:**
- After the FTD, watch how leading stocks behave: are CANSLIM-quality stocks breaking out of sound bases on heavy volume? If yes, the FTD is being confirmed by the "troops" (individual stocks).
- If top stocks are not breaking out or breakouts are failing within days, the FTD may be a false signal regardless of what the index does.
- A second FTD (another big up day on higher volume) within 1-2 weeks of the first adds confirmation.

**When FTDs fail:**
- If the market accumulates distribution days (see below) shortly after the FTD, it is likely a false signal
- If the index undercuts the low of the rally attempt after the FTD, the rally has failed
- Early FTDs in a severe bear market (Day 4-5 of the first rally attempt off a major low) have a higher failure rate than FTDs that occur after the market has had time to stabilize

### Distribution Day Counting -- Identifying Market Tops

**Definition:** A distribution day occurs when a major index (Nasdaq or S&P 500) declines on volume higher than the previous session. This indicates institutional selling (distribution).

**The mechanical rules:**
- A distribution day requires: (a) index closes DOWN, and (b) volume is HIGHER than the previous session
- Stalling days (the index closes up but near the session low on heavy volume, making negligible progress) also count as distribution
- Accumulating 4-5 distribution days within a 25-trading-day window = the market is under heavy institutional selling pressure and the uptrend is in jeopardy
- 6-7 distribution days can be tolerated IF most of the declines are small in magnitude (less than 0.2%). Several large declines (1%+) are far more damaging than many small ones.

**Distribution day removal rules:**
A distribution day falls off the count in three ways:
1. **Time expiration:** After 25 trading sessions, the distribution day expires and is removed from the count
2. **Price rally:** If the index rises 6% (intraday) from the close on the distribution day, that distribution day is removed
3. These removal rules prevent stale distribution from artificially inflating the count

**How to interpret:**
- 0-2 distribution days: Healthy uptrend. Full exposure permitted.
- 3-4 distribution days: Uptrend under pressure. Begin raising stops, taking partial profits, and limiting new purchases.
- 5+ distribution days within 25 sessions: High probability the market is topping. Reduce exposure significantly.
- Clustering: Multiple distribution days within a short span (3 within a week) is more bearish than distribution days spread over several weeks.

### Power Trend -- The Most Bullish Signal

The Power Trend concept was developed by IBD's Market School team (Mike Webster, Justin Nielsen, Charles Harris) while working with O'Neil. It identifies the strongest possible market environment.

**Power Trend starts when ALL of these conditions are met simultaneously:**
1. The index's intraday low has been above the 21-day EMA for at least 10 consecutive days
2. The 21-day EMA has been above the 50-day SMA for at least 5 consecutive days
3. The 50-day SMA is in an uptrend (sloping up)
4. The index closes up for the day

**Power Trend ends when:**
- The 21-day EMA crosses below the 50-day SMA, OR
- The index closes 10% below a recent high AND below the 50-day SMA (circuit breaker)

**Significance:**
- During a Power Trend, the market is in an exceptionally strong uptrend. This is the environment where breakouts have the highest success rate and the biggest winners emerge.
- O'Neil recommended maximum exposure (80-100%) during Power Trends.
- Power Trends are relatively rare -- they do not occur during every bull market and their presence signals extraordinary institutional demand.

### Market Exposure Model

O'Neil's system ties portfolio exposure directly to market condition:

| Market State | Recommended Exposure | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Confirmed Uptrend + Power Trend | 80-100% | Aggressively buy best CANSLIM breakouts. Full position sizing. |
| Confirmed Uptrend | 60-80% | Buy breakouts from proper bases. Normal position sizing. |
| Uptrend Under Pressure | 25-50% | Halt new buys (or limit to exceptional setups). Tighten stops. Take partial profits. |
| Market in Correction | 0-25% | Move to cash. No new buys. Prepare watchlists for next uptrend. |
| Rally Attempt (pre-FTD) | 0-10% | Stay in cash. Watch for FTD. Build watchlists. |

## 6. The Selling System

O'Neil considered selling the hardest part of investing. His system addresses both offensive sells (taking profits) and defensive sells (cutting losses).

### Offensive Sells -- Taking Profits

**The 20-25% profit rule:**
- After studying hundreds of market leaders, O'Neil found that most stocks make their first meaningful move 20-25% from a proper breakout before pausing or correcting
- Take at least partial profits when a stock reaches 20-25% above the pivot point
- Exception: if the stock is acting exceptionally well and the market is strong, hold for a larger gain
- Practical application: sell 1/3 to 1/2 of the position at 20-25%, hold the rest with a tighter stop

**The 8-week hold rule (exception to the profit-taking rule):**
- If a stock gains 20%+ within 1-3 weeks of breaking out from a proper base, it is potentially an exceptional winner
- HOLD for at least 8 weeks from the breakout date, regardless of short-term fluctuations
- After 8 weeks, evaluate: if the stock is still acting well (above the 10-week moving average, relative strength making new highs), continue to hold
- This rule forces the investor to sit through normal volatility in what may become a 100-500%+ winner
- "Big stock moves take time. Most investors sell too early because they can't sit through the normal pullbacks."

**Climax top signals (sell immediately when these appear):**
- **Exhaustion gap:** Stock gaps up to new highs on the heaviest volume in its entire run, then reverses and closes near the session low. This is the final burst of buying euphoria.
- **Climax run:** Price surges 25-50% in 1-3 weeks on very heavy volume after an already extended advance (stock has been running for months). This is the terminal blow-off.
- **Largest daily price spread:** The single largest daily price range since the beginning of the advance often marks the top.
- **Railroad tracks:** Two days of large price range with nearly equal opens and closes, but one up and one down (looks like two parallel vertical lines). Signals reversal.
- **Upper channel line:** If the stock reaches the upper boundary of a long-term rising channel after an extended advance, expect reversal or significant correction.

### Defensive Sells -- Cutting Losses

**The 7-8% stop loss rule:**
- If a stock declines 7-8% below your purchase price, sell immediately. NO EXCEPTIONS.
- This is O'Neil's single most important risk management rule.
- The math: if you cut every loss at 7-8% and take gains at 20-25%, you can be wrong on 3 out of 4 trades and still break even. You only need to be right on 1 in 3 trades to make money.
- "The whole secret to winning big in the stock market is not to be right all the time, but to lose the least amount possible when you're wrong."

**Other defensive sell signals:**
- Stock closes back below the breakout pivot point on heavy volume (failed breakout)
- Stock drops below the 50-day (10-week) moving average on heavy volume after a sustained advance
- Relative strength line starts trending down (the stock is underperforming the market even if price holds)
- Stock makes a new high on declining volume (exhaustion -- institutions are not supporting the advance)
- Stock drops below the 200-day moving average
- The general market shifts from "Confirmed Uptrend" to "Market in Correction" (M factor override)
- Wide and loose price action develops after a sustained advance (erratic swings = institutional uncertainty)

### Key Sell Signals Checklist

| Signal | Type | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Down 7-8% from buy point | Defensive | Sell immediately, no exceptions |
| Failed breakout (close below pivot on volume) | Defensive | Sell same day or next morning |
| Break below 50-day MA on heavy volume | Defensive | Sell |
| RS line trending down | Defensive | Sell or tighten stop |
| New high on declining volume | Warning | Tighten stop |
| Market shifts to Correction | Defensive | Reduce exposure, sell weakest positions first |
| Up 20-25% from pivot | Offensive | Take at least partial profits |
| Up 20%+ in 1-3 weeks | Offensive | Invoke 8-week hold rule |
| Exhaustion gap on heaviest volume ever | Offensive | Sell into strength |
| Climax run (25-50% surge in 1-3 weeks) | Offensive | Sell into strength |
| Largest daily range since start of advance | Warning | Sell or tighten stop significantly |

## 7. Position Sizing and Portfolio Management

### Portfolio Concentration

O'Neil was firmly against over-diversification:
- "Over-diversification is a hedge for ignorance."
- Recommended holding no more than 8-10 stocks for a portfolio under $500K. Fewer is better if conviction is high.
- For smaller portfolios ($50-100K), 4-5 positions is optimal.
- The logic: if you have done the work to identify the best CANSLIM stocks, spreading capital across 20-30 names dilutes your best ideas and guarantees mediocre returns.

### Pyramiding into Winners

O'Neil's position-building approach:
- **Initial buy:** Purchase at the pivot point. Initial position should be significant (e.g., 50% of intended full position).
- **Add on confirmation:** If the stock advances 2-3% above the pivot and acts well, add another 30% of the intended position.
- **Final add:** Add the remaining 20% if the stock continues to advance within 5% of the pivot.
- **Never add more than 5% above the pivot.** If you missed the buy zone, wait for the next base or pullback to proper support.
- The result: your largest positions become your best-performing stocks (you pyramid into strength), and your smallest positions are the ones that triggered your 7-8% stop (you are automatically sized out of losers).

### Cutting Losers Fast

- Sell your worst performers first. Keep your best performers longest.
- Never average down. Buying more of a declining stock increases your exposure to a losing position.
- If a stock hits the 7-8% stop, sell and move on. Do not re-enter the same stock unless it forms a new proper base and breaks out again.
- "I make it a rule to never lose more than 7 percent on any stock I buy. If a stock drops 7 percent below my purchase price, I will automatically sell at the market -- no ifs, ands, or buts."

### The 2% Capital Risk Rule

O'Neil also applied a portfolio-level risk rule: never let a single trade result in more than a 2% loss of total portfolio value. Combined with the 7-8% stock-level stop, this determines maximum position size.

Example: $100,000 portfolio. Maximum loss on any trade = $2,000 (2%). If buying at $50 with an 8% stop ($46), risk per share = $4. Maximum shares = 500 ($25,000 position, or 25% of portfolio). This naturally limits position sizes and enforces concentration.

### Handling a Hot Streak

- When 2-3 stocks are working well and moving up strongly, let them run. Add to your best positions, not your worst.
- When everything is working, it typically means the market is in a confirmed uptrend or power trend. Stay aggressive.
- When several positions hit their stops in rapid succession, it typically means the market is topping. Reduce exposure even before the formal market status changes.

## 8. Case Studies -- Stocks CANSLIM Caught Early

### Cisco Systems (1990-1994)

- **C:** Quarterly EPS gains of 150% and 155% in the two quarters ending October 1990
- **A:** Rapid annual earnings growth from a dominant position in networking equipment
- **N:** New product: the network router, enabling the internet backbone. A transformational technology.
- **S:** Relatively small float at the time; stock was not yet widely known
- **L:** RS rating in the high 80s/90s as the stock began outperforming everything in tech
- **I:** Smart money (technology-focused mutual funds) was accumulating
- **M:** The 1990 bear market ended and a follow-through day confirmed a new uptrend
- **Result:** Rose ~2,000% from November 1990 to March 1994. 75,000% from 1990 to 2000.

### Google (2004 IPO)

## 9. O'Neil vs Lynch vs Minervini -- Comparative Analysis

| Dimension | O'Neil (CANSLIM) | Lynch (GARP) | Minervini (SEPA) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Philosophy** | Systematic growth momentum | Growth at a reasonable price | Precise momentum trading |
| **Holding period** | Weeks to months (rarely years) | Months to years | Weeks to months |
| **Valuation** | Irrelevant (great stocks look "expensive" before their biggest moves) | Central (PEG ratio < 1) | Secondary to price action |
| **Chart patterns** | Cup-with-handle, double bottom, flat base, etc. (broad toolkit) | Does not use technical analysis | VCP (Volatility Contraction Pattern) -- more specific/refined |
| **Market timing** | Follow-through days, distribution days (mechanical system) | Stays invested through cycles ("time in market") | Similar to O'Neil but with tighter entry precision |
| **Sell discipline** | 7-8% stop loss, 20-25% profit rule | Sell when fundamentals deteriorate | 7-8% stop loss, similar to O'Neil |
| **Diversification** | Concentrated (8-10 stocks max) | Broad (Lynch held 1,400+ stocks at Magellan) | Concentrated (similar to O'Neil) |
| **Earnings focus** | Acceleration required (25%+ and accelerating) | Growth rate vs. P/E (PEG) | Acceleration required (similar to O'Neil) |
| **Cash position** | 0-100% depending on market | Always nearly fully invested | 0-100% depending on market |
| **Key difference from O'Neil** | -- | No market timing, values stocks differently, longer holding period | Refined O'Neil's patterns into tighter VCP; more emphasis on volatility contraction as confirmation |

**Key insight:** Minervini is O'Neil's most direct intellectual descendant. SEPA is essentially CANSLIM with (a) tighter pattern definition (VCP vs. broader base patterns), (b) more precise pivot point identification, and (c) explicit volatility contraction as a confirmation signal. Lynch is philosophically different -- he pays for growth but demands valuation discipline, ignores technicals, and stays invested through bear markets.

## 10. Common Misunderstandings About CANSLIM

### "CANSLIM is just momentum investing"
Wrong. CANSLIM requires both fundamental quality (earnings acceleration, high ROE, cash flow) AND technical confirmation (base pattern, volume, relative strength). Pure momentum systems buy stocks that are going up regardless of fundamentals. CANSLIM demands that the fundamental story justify the price action.

### "High P/E stocks are too expensive"
O'Neil explicitly argued that P/E ratio is a misleading indicator for growth stocks. In his model book studies, many of the greatest winners had above-average P/E ratios BEFORE their major advance -- and the P/E expanded further as the stock advanced. "Would you rather buy the stock that's cheap and going nowhere, or the one that's expensive and going up 200%?"

### "You should buy on pullbacks / dips"
O'Neil's system buys at the pivot point -- the high of the handle or base, not on a pullback. Buying on dips means buying weakness. Buying at the pivot means buying confirmed strength. The pivot is the point where the stock's behavior proves that accumulation has overcome distribution.

### "The 7-8% stop is too tight"
The math says otherwise. With a 7-8% max loss and a 20-25% target gain, you need to be right only about 1 in 3 trades to make money. The tight stop prevents any single loss from becoming catastrophic. O'Neil's core insight: the MAGNITUDE of losses matters more than the FREQUENCY of losses.

### "CANSLIM doesn't work in bear markets"
Correct -- and that is by design. The M factor tells you to go to cash in bear markets. CANSLIM is not an all-weather strategy. It is designed to be aggressive in bull markets and defensive (cash) in bear markets. The system explicitly tells you when NOT to invest.

### "Just screen for CANSLIM stocks and buy them"
Screening is step 1. The screen identifies candidates. But the investor must then (a) confirm a proper base pattern, (b) wait for the breakout at the pivot on heavy volume, (c) verify the market is in a confirmed uptrend, and (d) apply proper position sizing and risk management. A CANSLIM screen without pattern recognition and market timing is incomplete.

### "Distribution days predict crashes"
Distribution days predict the probability of a market top, not a crash. A cluster of distribution days means institutional selling is increasing, which raises the probability that the uptrend will fail. It does not mean a crash is imminent. Sometimes the market transitions from uptrend to sideways (a correction in time rather than price). The system tells you to reduce exposure, not to short the market.

### "Averaging down helps lower your cost basis"
O'Neil was unequivocal: NEVER average down. Buying more of a declining stock increases your exposure to a losing position. The correct action is to sell when the stock hits the 7-8% stop and redeploy capital into a stock that IS working. "Your first loss is your best loss."

## 11. How to Apply to Korean Stocks

CANSLIM is designed for the U.S. market, but its principles are universal. Adaptation for the Korean market:

**Earnings (C and A):**
- Korean quarterly earnings cycle: January, April, July, October earnings seasons
- Use the same 25%+ quarterly EPS growth threshold
- Watch for acceleration patterns quarter over quarter
- Korean accounting standards (K-IFRS) are compatible with the analysis

**Relative Strength (L):**
- Calculate RS relative to KOSPI (for large caps) or KOSDAQ (for growth/small caps)
- The 197 industry group methodology can be approximated using WICS sector classification
- Korean "tema" (themes) can proxy for industry group analysis -- find leaders within hot themes
- Naver Finance and KRX provide relative performance data

**Institutional Sponsorship (I):**
- Korean equivalent: track foreign investor net buying (oegugin sunmaesu / 외국인 순매수)
- Foreign institutional buying trending up = strong sponsorship signal
- National Pension Service (NPS) holdings and changes are a proxy for institutional quality
- Track fund flow data from Korean mutual funds specializing in growth stocks

**Supply and Demand (S):**
- Small float + Korean retail interest can create explosive Stage 2 moves
- KOSDAQ stocks with small floats are particularly responsive to supply/demand dynamics
- Watch for heavy volume breakouts above resistance levels

**Market Direction (M):**
- Apply follow-through days to KOSPI or KOSDAQ index
- Distribution day counting: same methodology using KOSPI/KOSDAQ volume
- Apply diligently: Korean market drawdowns are severe and fast (the KOSPI has experienced 40-50%+ drawdowns multiple times)
- The Korean market is heavily influenced by foreign capital flows, semiconductor cycle, and China/U.S. trade dynamics -- these can override individual stock fundamentals

**Chart Patterns:**
- Cup-with-handle patterns appear frequently in Korean leading stocks
- The same base pattern rules apply (depth, duration, volume, handle characteristics)
- KOSDAQ growth stocks often form tighter, shorter-duration bases due to higher retail participation and momentum

**Practical tools for Korean CANSLIM:**
- Naver Finance: relative strength data, foreign/institutional trading data, earnings data
- KRX data: volume, float, institutional flows
- MarketSmith (Korean version if available) or manual screening using the criteria above
