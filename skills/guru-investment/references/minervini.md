## 2. Stage Analysis -- Advanced

Adapted from Stan Weinstein's four-stage model, Minervini refined it into the foundation of
all trade decisions. A stock must be in Stage 2 to be purchased. No exceptions.

### The Four Stages

| Stage | Name | Moving Averages | Price Action | Volume | Action |
|-------|------|----------------|-------------|--------|--------|
| 1 | Basing / Neglect | 200-day MA flat, price oscillates around it | Sideways chop, no trend | Drying up, low interest | Watch list only. Prepare. |
| 2 | Advancing | Price > 50 > 150 > 200-day MA, all rising | Higher highs, higher lows | Expands on up days | **BUY ZONE** |
| 3 | Topping / Distribution | MAs converge, 200-day flattens | Wide and loose, volatile | Heavy volume on declines | SELL. Do not initiate. |
| 4 | Declining / Capitulation | Price < declining 200-day MA | Lower highs, lower lows | Panic selling spikes | Avoid entirely. Never average down. |

### Transition Signals (Real-Time Identification)

**Stage 1 to Stage 2 transition clues:**
- The 200-day MA stops declining and begins to flatten, then slowly turns up
- Price makes a decisive move above the 200-day MA on above-average volume
- The 50-day MA crosses above the 150-day, then the 150-day crosses above the 200-day
- Price is at least 25-30% above its 52-week low
- Relative strength starts improving vs. the market index

**Stage 2 to Stage 3 transition warnings:**
- The 200-day MA begins to flatten after a long advance
- Price starts oscillating above and below the 50-day MA with wide swings
- Volume increases on down days while up-day volume fades
- Moving averages start converging (50/150/200 cluster together)
- Relative strength deteriorates while price may still be near highs

### Common Mistakes in Stage Analysis
- Buying Stage 1 bottoms prematurely ("it looks cheap") -- dead money
- Confusing a Stage 3 rally attempt with renewed Stage 2 -- this is how you buy tops
- Assuming all Stage 4 stocks are "oversold bargains" -- they usually keep falling
- Ignoring the 200-day MA direction -- the single most important tell

## 4. Fundamental Criteria -- Advanced

Minervini insists that "really only three things matter: earnings, sales, and margins."
Getting good at deciphering those three represents 90% of fundamental analysis.

### 4a. Earnings Acceleration Patterns

More than 90% of superperformance stocks showed earnings acceleration before or during their
major price advance. Acceleration means EPS growth is *increasing* quarter over quarter:

**What "good" acceleration looks like:**
- Q1: +10%, Q2: +18%, Q3: +30%, Q4: +50% -- each quarter's YoY growth rate is higher
- Minimum: Most recent quarter EPS growth >= 25% YoY, ideally 30-50%+
- The stock should also be beating analyst estimates (positive earnings surprises)

**What "bad" deceleration looks like:**
- Q1: +40%, Q2: +35%, Q3: +25% -- growth rate is declining even though earnings are "growing"
- Deceleration warns that the fundamental engine is fading -- the stock is vulnerable

**"Code 33" -- the holy grail:** Three consecutive quarters of accelerating earnings, sales,
AND profit margins simultaneously. This triple acceleration is extremely rare and powerful.

### 4b. Revenue Quality Checks

Revenue growth confirms that earnings growth comes from genuine business expansion,
not cost-cutting or accounting tricks. Key checks:
- Quarterly revenue growth >= 15% YoY (ideally 20%+)
- Revenue growth should be accelerating or at minimum sustained
- If earnings grow faster than revenue, verify it is from margin expansion, not one-time items
- Declining revenue with growing earnings is a red flag -- unsustainable

### 4c. Margin Expansion as a Leading Indicator

Rising operating margins signal pricing power and operating leverage -- the business is
scaling efficiently. Margin expansion often precedes the biggest moves because it means
each dollar of revenue produces more profit, creating an earnings multiplier effect.
- Watch for gross margins AND operating margins both expanding
- Revenue growth exceeding earnings growth = margin compression (concerning)
- Earnings growth exceeding revenue growth from genuine margin expansion = very bullish

### 4d. The Earnings Season Surprise Setup

Minervini pays particular attention to stocks that gap up on earnings, then build a
constructive base (VCP) during the weeks following. The gap-up proves institutional demand.
The subsequent consolidation digests the move. The breakout from that post-earnings base
can be explosive because:
1. Earnings growth is confirmed and recent
2. The next catalyst (following quarter's earnings) is weeks away
3. Institutions that missed the gap are waiting to accumulate on any pullback

## 5. Advanced Chart Patterns

### 5a. Volatility Contraction Pattern (VCP) -- The Complete Treatment

The VCP is Minervini's signature setup and the centerpiece of his technical analysis.

**The Concept:** After a stock advances, it pulls back and consolidates. Within that
consolidation, each successive contraction (pullback) should be *shallower* than the last,
and volume should *dry up* progressively. This shows that selling pressure is being exhausted
and supply is being absorbed by patient institutional buyers.

**Visual description (text-based):**

```
Price
  |     *
  |    * *         *
  |   *   *       * *       *
  |  *     *     *   *     * *     * <-- Pivot/Breakout
  | *       *   *     *   *   *   *
  |          * *       * *     * *
  |           *         *       *
  |
  |  [--T1--] [--T2--] [--T3--]  --> Contractions get tighter
  |  -25%      -12%     -5%        --> Depth decreases
  |  High Vol  Med Vol  Low Vol    --> Volume dries up
```

**Anatomy of a proper VCP:**
1. **Prior uptrend** -- The stock must be in a Stage 2 uptrend (not basing or declining)
2. **Multiple contractions** -- Typically 2-6 pullbacks, each shallower than the previous
   - Example progression: -25%, -15%, -8%, -4%
   - The final contraction is often 3-5% or less
3. **Volume signature** -- Volume dries up dramatically in the final contraction.
   3-5 consecutive days of extremely low volume near the pivot = sellers exhausted
4. **Tightness in the final contraction** -- Daily price ranges narrow; closes cluster
5. **The pivot point** -- The high of the final contraction; the breakout level
6. **Breakout volume** -- Must be at least 150% of average; ideally 200%+

**"Tennis ball action"** -- Quick, sharp recoveries after small pullbacks within the base
show underlying demand strength. The stock "bounces" like a tennis ball rather than sinking
like a stone.

**VCP Failure Modes:**
- Wide and loose action in later contractions (supply not absorbed)
- Volume stays high during pullbacks (heavy selling, not drying up)
- Breakout on weak volume (no institutional participation)
- Breakout immediately reverses (failed breakout -- cut loss immediately)
- Late-stage base (3rd or 4th base in the advance -- higher failure rate)
- Breaking below the low of the previous contraction (pattern invalidated)

### 5b. Power Play (Early-Stage Breakout)

The Power Play is Minervini's name for what is essentially a High Tight Flag:
- Stock surges 100%+ in under 8 weeks, often after a long period of dormancy
- The move is typically driven by a major fundamental catalyst (transformational earnings)
- Price then consolidates sideways for 3-6 weeks
- The consolidation is TIGHT: corrections no deeper than 20-25%
- Volume contracts substantially during the consolidation
- Breakout from the flag triggers the next leg up

**When it works best:** Very early in a new major advance, often the first base after a
stock emerges from Stage 1 obscurity. The 100%+ move proves massive institutional demand.
The tight flag proves they are not selling.

**Caution:** This is an advanced pattern. The 100%+ prior move can scare traders into
thinking the stock is "extended." The key is recognizing that with transformational
fundamentals, the first move is just the market waking up.

### 5c. Cheat Setup (Early Entry Within a Base)

The "Cheat" is a lower pivot that forms within a base before the handle develops:
- While a standard VCP/cup-with-handle has you buy at the breakout of the handle,
  the Cheat entry occurs when price makes a small pause (5-10% depth) at a lower level
  within the right side of the base
- You buy as price clears the high of this small pause area
- This gives a lower-risk entry (tighter stop) and a head start before the "official" pivot
- Low cheat entries can be up to 8% below the 50-day MA
- Commonly appears during general market corrections when stocks bottom before the index

**Advantage:** Lower entry point means tighter stop-loss, which means you can take a larger
position size for the same dollar risk.

### 5d. High Tight Flag

Criteria (per O'Neil and refined by Minervini):
- Price advance of 25-100%+ in 4-8 weeks or less
- Consolidation: 1-3 weeks, pullback no more than 10-20%
- Volume decreases sharply during consolidation
- Breakout above the flag's high on expanding volume
- One of the most powerful and profitable patterns when it works
- Also one of the most commonly misidentified -- the prior advance must be genuine, not a
  low-float squeeze or manipulated move

### 5e. Identifying Late-Stage Bases (Avoid These)

Base counting is critical. Each time a stock breaks out, consolidates, and breaks out again,
it forms a new base in the sequence.

| Base Count | Risk Level | Guidance |
|-----------|-----------|---------|
| 1st base | Lowest risk | Best entry -- fresh breakout from Stage 1 to Stage 2 |
| 2nd base | Low risk | Still excellent -- the advance is confirmed but not obvious |
| 3rd base | Moderate risk | Acceptable but be cautious -- the move is now well-known |
| 4th+ base | High risk | AVOID -- prone to failed breakouts and deep corrections |

**Why late bases fail:** By the 4th base, the stock's story is widely known, early investors
are sitting on large gains, and the pool of new buyers is depleted. The "smart money" that
accumulated in base 1-2 begins distributing to retail latecomers.

## 6. Risk Management -- The Complete System

This is the most important section. Minervini: "Risk management is the single most
important factor in trading success."

### 6a. The Math of Asymmetric Stops

Losses and gains are NOT symmetrical. This geometric reality is why small losses are critical:

| Loss | Gain Needed to Recover | Difficulty |
|------|----------------------|------------|
| -5% | +5.3% | Easy |
| -8% | +8.7% | Manageable |
| -10% | +11.1% | Still OK |
| -15% | +17.6% | Getting harder |
| -20% | +25.0% | Significantly harder |
| -25% | +33.3% | Very difficult |
| -33% | +50.0% | Extremely difficult |
| -50% | +100.0% | Nearly impossible for most |
| -75% | +300.0% | Virtually impossible |

At 7-8% max loss, recovery requires only ~8-9% -- easily achievable with one good trade.
This is why the stop-loss is non-negotiable: it keeps you in the zone where recovery is trivial.

**Minervini's key insight on optimal math:**
"You gain 100% and lose 50% ten times, you break even. You gain 10% and lose 5% ten times,
you make 71.64%. The key to big returns is optimal math, not the biggest wins."

### 6b. Position Sizing Formula

**The formula:** Position Size = (Portfolio Risk %) / (Stop-Loss %)

**Example with 1% portfolio risk, 8% stop:**
- Portfolio: $100,000
- Dollar risk per trade: $100,000 x 0.01 = $1,000
- Stop distance: 8%
- Position size: $1,000 / 0.08 = $12,500 (12.5% of portfolio)

**Example with tighter 5% stop (Cheat entry):**
- Dollar risk: $1,000
- Position size: $1,000 / 0.05 = $20,000 (20% of portfolio)

This is the mathematical advantage of buying at the pivot: the closer your entry to the
stop level, the larger position you can take for the same dollar risk.

**Typical concentration:** 5-8 positions. Minervini is NOT a hyper-diversifier. He
concentrates in his best ideas.

### 6c. Portfolio Heat (Total Open Risk)

Portfolio heat = the sum of all position risks if every stop is hit simultaneously.
- Maximum portfolio heat: 5-8% of total portfolio at any time
- With 5 positions each risking 1.5% = 7.5% total heat
- If portfolio heat gets too high, do not add new positions until existing ones are in profit
  and stops have been raised

### 6d. The 2-to-1 Minimum Reward/Risk Ratio

Before entering any trade, the potential reward must be at least 2x the risk:
- If your stop is 8%, you need a reasonable expectation of at least 16% upside
- This ensures that even with a 50% win rate, you are profitable:
  (50% x $16 gain) - (50% x $8 loss) = +$4 average per trade

**Actual target:** Minervini aims for 3:1 or better on his best setups.

### 6e. When to Tighten Stops vs. Give Room

**Tighten stops when:**
- The stock has gained 2-3x your initial risk (e.g., up 15-20% with an 8% stop)
- Move stop to breakeven, then trail it
- The stock is approaching obvious resistance or round numbers
- Earnings are imminent and you want to protect gains
- Market conditions are deteriorating

**Give room when:**
- The stock is within the first few days of a breakout (allow normal volatility)
- The stock is in a strong uptrend with no distribution signals
- Volume on pullbacks is light and orderly

## 7. Selling Framework

Knowing when to sell is as important as knowing when to buy. Minervini has specific rules
for both offensive (selling into strength) and defensive (cutting losses) selling.

### 7a. Selling into Strength -- Climax Signals

A climax top occurs after a prolonged advance when the stock makes its largest and fastest
price gain on its heaviest volume. Signs to sell into:

- **Exhaustion gap:** Stock gaps up dramatically on huge volume after an extended advance
- **Widest daily spread:** The biggest single-day price range of the entire move
- **Heaviest volume:** The highest volume day of the advance occurs near the top
- **Parabolic acceleration:** Price goes vertical -- the last 10-20% of the move happens
  in just a few days
- **Churning:** Heavy volume but no upward price progress (institutions selling into demand)
- **Railroad tracks:** A large up day followed immediately by a large down day on heavy volume

### 7b. Trailing Stop Methodology

- After a stock gains 2-3x your initial risk, move stop to breakeven
- Use the 10-day or 20-day MA as a trailing stop for fast-moving stocks
- Use the 50-day MA as a trailing stop for stocks in steady uptrends
- A close below the trailing stop on above-average volume = exit signal
- Never move a stop DOWN -- stops only move up

### 7c. Time-Based Stops

If a stock does not make meaningful progress within a reasonable time after purchase, sell it:
- If a breakout stalls and makes no upward progress within 2-3 weeks, consider selling
- Capital tied up in dead trades is a hidden cost (opportunity cost)
- "If it doesn't move, move on"

### 7d. Warning Signs of a Topping Stock

- Largest daily price spread on highest volume (climax signals above)
- Multiple distribution days (heavy-volume declines) within a short period
- Price breaks below the 50-day MA on heavy volume and cannot recover
- The 50-day MA begins to flatten or turn down
- Relative strength deteriorates while price is still near highs
- Wide and loose weekly price action replacing tight, controlled advances

## 8. Market Direction Assessment

### 8a. How Minervini Reads the General Market

Unlike most traders who start with index analysis, Minervini takes a **bottoms-up approach:**
"I don't really do a whole lot of general market analysis. 90% of my work is all stock work.
If there are a lot of stocks setting up, I'm bullish on the market."

He gauges market health by:
- The number and quality of stocks meeting his Trend Template
- How breakouts are performing -- are they following through or failing?
- The ratio of stocks making new highs vs. new lows
- Whether leading stocks are acting well (holding gains, building constructive bases)

### 8b. Exposure Rules by Market Condition

| Market Condition | Signals | Exposure Level |
|-----------------|---------|---------------|
| Strong uptrend | Many setups, breakouts working, breadth expanding | 80-100% invested, larger positions |
| Moderate uptrend | Fewer setups, mixed breakout results | 50-70% invested, normal positions |
| Uncertain / Choppy | Breakouts failing, mixed signals | 20-40% invested, pilot positions only |
| Correction / Downtrend | Few/no setups, stops getting hit, breadth contracting | 0-20% invested, mostly cash |
| Bear market | Indices below declining 200-day MA, stocks breaking down | 100% cash. Wait. |

### 8c. Progressive Exposure -- Trading Size with Confidence

Minervini modulates his aggression based on recent trading performance:
1. **Start from cash with pilot positions** -- small test buys to get market feedback
2. **If pilots work**, increase to half-positions, then full positions
3. **If pilots fail**, reduce size further or go to cash entirely
4. **After a winning streak**, increase position sizes because your account equity
   (and confidence based on market confirmation) is growing
5. **After a losing streak**, cut back to minimum size until results improve

**Position scaling levels:**
- Pilot buy: ~25% of normal position size
- Half position: 50%
- Three-quarter position: 75%
- Full position: 100%
- Overweight (conviction + track record hot): 125-150%

This is the opposite of gambling psychology (increasing bets after losses). Minervini
increases exposure when the market proves him right and decreases when it proves him wrong.

### 8d. Stockpicker's Market vs. Rising Tide Market

- **Rising tide market:** Broad-based rally, most stocks go up. Easier to make money but
  winners are less differentiated. Can be more aggressive with position count.
- **Stockpicker's market:** Narrow leadership, only select stocks advance while the average
  stock goes nowhere. Must be highly selective. Concentrate in the best names.
  This is where SEPA's selectivity provides the biggest edge.

## 9. The Psychology of Winning

Minervini considers psychology his most important contribution. "Think & Trade Like a
Champion" and "Mindset Secrets for Winning" devote hundreds of pages to this topic.

### 9a. The Champion Mindset Framework

Minervini studied Olympic athletes, world-class coaches, and peak performers across
disciplines to identify what separates champions from the competent:

**Key principles:**
- **Self-image is a performance regulator:** You will always perform in accordance with
  your self-image. If you see yourself as a mediocre trader, you will unconsciously
  sabotage winning trades and tolerate losing ones. Change the self-image first.
- **The "like you" zone:** People create a comfort zone of outcomes they believe are
  "like them." A trader who believes they can make 20% a year will unconsciously take
  profits too early or increase risk recklessly when exceeding that level.
- **Beliefs create confirmation bias:** Strongly held beliefs filter information.
  If you believe "the market is rigged," you will find evidence everywhere. Replace
  limiting beliefs with empowering ones grounded in evidence and process.

### 9b. Process Over Outcome

- Judge yourself on whether you followed the process, NOT on the result of individual trades
- A losing trade executed according to the plan is a GOOD trade
- A winning trade that violated the rules is a BAD trade (it reinforces bad habits)
- Keep a trading journal: record the setup, entry, stop, target, and whether you followed
  every rule. Review weekly.

### 9c. Handling Losing Streaks

- Losing streaks are inevitable and normal -- even Minervini has them
- The response: reduce position size (progressive exposure in reverse)
- Conduct post-analysis: "What went wrong? Was it my execution or the market environment?"
- Do NOT change your system during a drawdown -- that is when discipline matters most
- Setbacks are data, not identity. "Failure is not a reality but a perception."

### 9d. The Mental Rehearsal Technique

Every morning before the market opens, Minervini mentally rehearses:
- "If stock X gaps up, I will do Y"
- "If my stop is hit, I will sell immediately without hesitation"
- "If the breakout fails, I will cut the loss and move on"
- "If I get a climax signal, I will sell into strength"

By pre-programming responses to every scenario, he eliminates real-time emotional
decision-making. When the event occurs, he executes the rehearsed plan automatically.
This is not positive thinking -- it is preparation for BOTH good and bad outcomes.

### 9e. Why Most Traders Fail

Minervini identifies the core failure as emotional, not intellectual:
- **Fear of missing out (FOMO):** Chasing extended stocks, buying without a proper setup
- **Fear of loss:** Refusing to cut losses, hoping for recovery
- **Fear of being wrong:** Not taking a trade because "what if it fails"
- **Ego:** Averaging down to avoid admitting a mistake
- **Lack of accountability:** Blaming the market, news, manipulation -- never themselves

"Being wrong is acceptable, but staying wrong is totally unacceptable."

## 12. Common Misunderstandings

**"Minervini buys expensive stocks."**
No. He buys stocks near 52-week highs, but "expensive" refers to valuation, not price.
A stock at a 52-week high with accelerating earnings may be the cheapest it will ever be
relative to its future earnings power.

**"The VCP is just a pattern -- draw the lines and buy."**
No. Without confirming fundamentals, Stage 2 status, proper volume signature, and base
count, a "VCP shape" on a chart is meaningless. Context is everything.

**"7-8% stops mean you get stopped out all the time."**
If you buy at proper VCP pivot points with tight contractions, the stop-loss is placed
just below the pattern's logical support. Stocks that break out properly rarely pull back
8% from the pivot. Frequent stop-outs indicate poor entry selection, not a flawed stop rule.

**"You need a high win rate to succeed."**
No. Minervini's system works with a win rate of 40-50% because average gains far exceed
average losses. The math: 50% win rate x 15% average gain / 50% x 7% average loss =
net expectancy of +4% per trade.

**"Just screen for the Trend Template and buy whatever passes."**
The Trend Template is a filter, not a buy signal. After passing the template, you still need
a proper base pattern, volume confirmation, and fundamental quality before buying.

**"Psychology doesn't matter if you have good rules."**
The rules are useless without the psychological capacity to follow them. Cutting an 8% loss
on paper is simple. Clicking "sell" when your money is on the line and your ego screams
"it will come back" is where 90% of traders fail.

  fundamental criteria.
- **Stop-loss adjustment:** Korean stocks have higher overnight gap risk due to market hours
  and global overnight moves. Adjust stops slightly wider: 8-10% rather than 7-8%.
  Compensate by reducing position size proportionally.
- **VCP on Korean charts:** The pattern works identically. Korean charts show VCPs on Naver
  Finance, KRX, and most Korean brokerage platforms. Look for the same contraction and
  volume dry-up characteristics.
- **Liquidity filter:** Avoid stocks with daily volume below 1 billion KRW average.
  Illiquid stocks make it impossible to execute proper entries and exits.
- **Samsung/Hyundai trap:** Large-cap Korean conglomerates rarely produce SEPA setups.
  The methodology works best on growth-stage companies, not mature conglomerates.
